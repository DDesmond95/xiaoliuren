Beginner Guide

Start Here

Xiao Liu Ren (小六壬) is a traditional Chinese divination method that uses three time-based inputs: lunar month, lunar day, and Earthly Branch hour.

This website is designed so that someone who knows nothing about Xiao Liu Ren can still use it. You can calculate manually, use Gregorian dates and clock time, explore a generated dataset, and read explanations about the cultural ideas behind the system.

Quick Summary

  • Six results in a repeating cycle
  • Can use current time or a selected time
  • Can convert Gregorian date to lunar date
  • Includes charts, filters, and explanations
  • Includes science and psychology notes

What are the six results?

大安 留连 速喜 赤口 小吉 空亡

These six names form the repeating outcome cycle used in this site.

Calculation Formula

(month + day + hour − 3) mod 6

Here, “month” means lunar month, “day” means lunar day, and “hour” means the Earthly Branch hour number.

The result index from the formula maps to one of the six outcomes in a fixed order:

  1. 大安
  2. 留连
  3. 速喜
  4. 赤口
  5. 小吉
  6. 空亡

        Concepts and Terminology

        Learning

        This section explains the main concepts behind Xiao Liu Ren in beginner-friendly language.

        Xiao Liu Ren is a compact divination method that assigns one of six outcomes to a given moment or selected time using a cyclical calculation.

        A lunar date comes from the traditional Chinese lunisolar calendar. It does not match the Gregorian calendar month and day directly.

        The day is divided into twelve named branch periods, each covering roughly two modern clock hours.

        • 子 — 23:00-00:59
        • 丑 — 01:00-02:59
        • 寅 — 03:00-04:59
        • 卯 — 05:00-06:59
        • 辰 — 07:00-08:59
        • 巳 — 09:00-10:59
        • 午 — 11:00-12:59
        • 未 — 13:00-14:59
        • 申 — 15:00-16:59
        • 酉 — 17:00-18:59
        • 戌 — 19:00-20:59
        • 亥 — 21:00-22:59

        大安

        Stable, calm, steady, broadly favorable.

        留连

        Delay, repetition, lingering, entanglement.

        速喜

        Quick movement, favorable news, speed.

        赤口

        Conflict, friction, disagreement, sharpness.

        小吉

        Moderately good, manageable benefit, support.

        空亡

        Emptiness, non-arrival, unreliability, uncertainty.

        Many traditional Chinese systems place time, change, and events into larger symbolic frameworks such as Yin-Yang and the Five Elements. This site explains those ideas as cultural context, not as scientific proof.

        Read results as symbolic prompts or cultural interpretations. Do not treat them as guaranteed predictions or as substitutes for professional medical, legal, or financial advice.

        Critical Thinking

        Science & Psychology

        This section explains why symbolic systems can feel meaningful, and why users should distinguish cultural practice from scientific proof.

        Pattern Seeking

        Humans naturally look for patterns. This can make symbolic systems feel personally meaningful, especially when dealing with uncertainty.

        Confirmation Bias

        People often remember “hits” and forget “misses.” This can make a divination method feel more accurate than it objectively is.

        Barnum Effect

        Broad or flexible statements can feel very personally accurate, especially when the reader is emotionally engaged.

        Decision Support vs Proof

        A symbolic system may help reflection, framing, or emotional processing, but that is not the same thing as scientific validation.

        Balanced Use

        This website treats Xiao Liu Ren as a cultural and symbolic system. It can be interesting, educational, and meaningful to some users, but it should not be confused with scientific evidence or used as the sole basis for high-stakes decisions.
